02 · Brand audit · current state Total Uptime — live site

What's on the site today

Current palette — functional, conservative.

The live Total Uptime palette is built around a mid-blue and a warm orange. It reads as competent enterprise infrastructure — but sits in the middle of the category and doesn't carry distinctive equity. There's no consistent accent or surface system; cards and bands rely on neutral greys.

Primary

Brand blue

#1F6AB5

Used on header, hero, logo. Slightly cool, lacks "voltage".

Accent CTA

Brand orange

#FF7A1A

All CTAs. Loud but reads as generic SaaS orange.

Surface

Neutral grey

#F4F6F8

Section background tint. No relationship to the brand colour.

Body text

Ink

#222222

Default copy. AAA contrast — keeping this.

What's working

  • · Information architecture is sound — products, solutions, pricing, resources, company.
  • · Typography (Inter / system) is clean and readable.
  • · Hero/section rhythm with alternating white & tinted bands is the right shape — just needs warmer execution.
  • · Stats messaging is sharp: 100% SLA, 24/7/365, six years uninterrupted.

What needs work

  • · Brand fades into the category. Mid-blue + orange is a default infrastructure pairing.
  • · No accent system. CTAs and "learn more" links use the same orange — no visual hierarchy between commercial and exploratory actions.
  • · Surface tokens are neutral, not branded. The page tints feel like a generic Bootstrap theme rather than a system.
  • · No tokenised category coding. Product families aren't visually distinguished in nav, tiles or pricing tables.
03 · Brand audit · proposed Adopting the Edgenexus visual direction

What we're proposing

Edgenexus tokens, applied wholesale.

Five jobs: brand, anchor, accent, support, surface. Every token earns its place — coral is reserved for the single primary CTA per view, cyan owns hero surfaces and link states, deep blue anchors typography and dark bands, mint signals success and category, sky tints the alternating section rhythm.

Why this works for Total Uptime

  • · Distinctive. The cyan + coral pairing is recognisable and rare in infrastructure.
  • · Hierarchical. Coral is rationed to commercial CTAs only — visual hierarchy by colour rules out ambiguity.
  • · Tokenised. Every UI element resolves to a named token, so design and dev stay in sync.
  • · Accessible. Anchor blue is AAA on white (9.44:1); contrast audit shipped alongside the system.

05 · Navigation audit · desktop Both pages, ≥ 1024 px viewports

Desktop nav findings

Largely OK — but Products needs room to breathe.

The current desktop main menu works. The issue is its future capacity: the new Products menu being planned has more line items than a single dropdown can carry, and the existing pattern doesn't support tiered grouping.

No issue

Header layout, utility row, sign-in / CTA

Standard pattern, predictable interaction, works to ~1280 px. Keeping it as the base.

Major

Single-column Products dropdown won't scale

The new Products menu groups items into three families (application delivery, DNS, network & security). A flat dropdown loses the hierarchy and crams items at > 8 entries.

Minor

DNS page sub-nav (desktop) hangs off the hero

The in-page anchor list reads as part of the hero rather than a persistent navigation aid. Once you scroll past it, finding it again requires scrolling back up.

Minor

Active state is colour-only

Hover and active states rely on text colour change alone. Adds aria-current + a pill background for clearer affordance.

Recommendation

  • · Adopt a 3-column mega-menu for Products, organised by category (cyan / mint / coral chip per column).
  • · Add a sticky pill rail for the DNS page in-page anchors — stays visible during scroll with scrollspy active state.
  • · Wire up scrollspy so the pill rail reflects current section.
06 · Navigation audit · mobile Critical — flagged in brief

The brief's number-two ask

DNS page mobile sub-nav — broken.

The client called this out in the brief, and the audit confirms it. The current DNS page sub-navigation can't be used on mobile at all: the sticky bar overflows the viewport, anchors stop firing once you scroll, and there's no recovery affordance.

Comprehensive DNS Infrastructure Platform.

Today

Sticky subnav overflows the viewport.

Six anchors crammed into a 375 px row. Horizontal scroll is broken, anchors lose target offset, and there's no visual indication of current section.

All mobile nav findings

  • · DNS subnav unusable — overflow, broken anchors, no recovery.
  • · Main hamburger has flat list — no grouping, no category coding, can't scan.
  • · No "where am I" affordance on long pages.
  • · CTAs hide below-the-fold in the drawer — commercial actions get buried.
  • · Tap targets < 44 px on dropdown chevrons.

Comprehensive DNS Infrastructure Platform.

Proposed

Floating "On this page" pill → bottom sheet.

Always-reachable pill sits bottom-right (clear of the thumb-zone safe area). Tap opens a 6-anchor bottom sheet with the primary CTA pinned to the bottom.

Why this pattern

  • · Always visible — survives any scroll position.
  • · Thumb-reachable — bottom-right of viewport.
  • · Familiar — bottom sheet pattern is standard on iOS & Android.
  • · Accessible — sheet uses role="dialog", traps focus, closes on ESC.
  • · Tap target ≥ 44 × 44 px (Apple HIG).
07 · Recommendations · patterns Adopted in the prototype

Patterns the reskin uses

Three nav patterns, one consistent system.

Each pattern below is built into the prototype, lives in src/input.css, and is documented in the brand system. They share tokens — colour, type, spacing, motion — so they feel like one product.

Pattern 01

Desktop mega-menu (Products)

Three columns, one per category. Coloured category chip + eyebrow per column. Each item carries a one-line description so users can scan rather than read.

Pattern 02

Mobile main drawer

Full-height drawer slides in from the right. Each primary item is a collapsible accordion (Alpine's x-collapse). Primary CTA is pinned to the bottom so it never scrolls off-screen. Tap targets are ≥ 44 px throughout.

Pattern 03

In-page navigation (desktop pill rail + mobile sheet)

Long product pages (DNS, future product pages) get a sticky pill rail on desktop and a floating "On this page" FAB on mobile. Both share the same anchor list and active state via shared Alpine store — change the anchors once and both surfaces update.
